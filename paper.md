## Abstract
Accurate forecasting of the global mean surface temperature anomalies is critical for understanding climate change and evaluating decarbonization pathways. However, current data-driven forecasting models often struggle to resolve the complex, non-linear interactions among coupled atmospheric variables while simultaneously obeying the rigid temporal periodicities (e.g., diurnal and annual cycles) dictated by Earth's orbital physics. To address these challenges, this study presents a geophysically-constrained, graph-based modeling framework designed to capture multi-scale climate periodicities and multivariate thermodynamic interactions. 

Instead of treating GSTA as a generic one-dimensional time series, we explicitly construct multi-period temporal graphs based on orbital priors to decouple intra-period and inter-period atmospheric dynamics. Concurrently, a variable-wise graph attention mechanism is formulated to dynamically infer the physical dependencies and energy correlations among diverse globally-averaged atmospheric drivers (e.g., solar radiation, cloud cover, and carbon dioxide concentrations). Our framework, evaluated on the ERA5 atmospheric reanalysis and NOAA carbon dioxide datasets, demonstrates strong fidelity in tracking historical temperature anomalies and outperforms conventional sequence models. Crucially, sensitivity analyses and hypothetical carbon reduction scenario projections indicate that the model realistically reflects the long-term mitigating effects of decarbonization on global warming, providing a data-driven tool for rapid climate policy assessment.

**Keywords:** Global mean surface temperature anomalies, Climate change, Multivariate time series, Graph Modeling.

## 1. Introduction
Advancements in weather and climate forecasting accuracy have, for decades, been primarily driven by a deeper comprehension of underlying physical mechanisms—a theory-driven paradigm [1]. The explosive growth of multi-source, multi-dimensional, and multi-period meteorological data necessitates that climate science develops robust methods to process these large and rapidly increasing datasets. This is crucial for providing more accurate, less uncertain, and physically consistent inferences in forecasting, modeling, and understanding the complex Earth climate system. Deep learning offers promising tools for constructing novel data-driven models of Earth system components, thereby leading to a deeper understanding of the Earth's climate [2].

Global mean surface temperature anomalies (GSTA) are a critical climate indicator [3], [4], [5], typically expressed as a temperature anomaly, which represents the deviation from an established average over a fixed reference period. Since 1850, Global mean surface temperature has been recorded as a time series through aggregated measurements from various sources, including weather stations, ships, buoys, and satellites. The Paris Agreement, a legally binding international treaty on climate change, aims to limit global warming to well below 2°C, with an aspirational target of 1.5°C, relative to pre-industrial levels [6]. Consequently, forecasting GSTA is essential as a time series prediction problem, given its reflection of the overarching trend of global warming and climate change. Over recent decades, numerous Earth System Models (ESMs) have been developed and have performed effectively in both weather and climate forecasting. However, these physics-based models often cannot fully account for all significant physical and chemical processes, particularly their complex interactions and feedback loops. Therefore, this study focuses on data-driven models to improve near-term climate predictions, specifically leveraging GSTA time series data.

Recently, interdisciplinary research spanning data science, information science, and geophysics has increasingly focused on leveraging artificial intelligence to enhance the precision of future temperature predictions. Numerous data-driven approaches have been utilized for time series forecasting, including deep learning models such as long short-term memory (LSTM) networks [7] and gated recurrent units (GRU) [8]. Nevertheless, directly extracting temporal features from temperature data with conventional deep learning models faces substantial limitations. This is particularly evident given the extensive temporal coverage of temperature time series and the influence of complex, interacting variables such as carbon emissions, solar radiation, and atmospheric humidity.

Recent advancements in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have positioned them as powerful tools for various meteorological forecasting tasks [9]. Specific graph-based models, including Graph Convolutional Networks (GCN) [10], Graph Attention Networks (GAT) [11], Graph Autoencoders [12], and Graph Diffusion Models [13], have demonstrated significant efficacy. Their capabilities span learning complex representations, predicting spatial distributions of meteorological variables, identifying critical nodes, and deriving insightful latent representations. These collective advancements underscore the pivotal role GNNs can play in supporting temperature time series forecasting.

This study leverages the fifth-generation atmospheric reanalysis dataset from the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts, known as ERA5 [14], which provides comprehensive global meteorological records from 1980 to 2024. This dataset encompasses various meteorological variables, including mean sea level pressure, 2-meter air temperature (T2m), northward and eastward 10-meter wind components (U10, V10), total cloud cover (TCC), total precipitation (TP), and surface solar radiation downwards (SSRD). This comprehensive range of features not only facilitates an in-depth analysis of temperature variability but also serves as a foundation for forecasting future temperature trends.

Mean sea level pressure (MSLP), as a fundamental variable in meteorology, exerts substantial influence on the formation and movement of weather systems, thereby impacting temperature dynamics. Northward and eastward wind components (U10, V10) capture atmospheric horizontal movement, offering insights into wind intensity and directionality. TCC, as a measure of the fraction of the sky covered by clouds, modulates Earth's radiation budget, affecting both incoming solar radiation and outgoing terrestrial radiation. TP affects surface moisture and temperature, and is closely intertwined with atmospheric thermodynamic processes. SSRD, as Earth’s primary energy source, directly drives surface temperature patterns and their fluctuations. Despite the demonstrated potential of deep learning techniques for meteorological forecasting, challenges persist in the field.

Recently, predicting the GSTA has been formulated as a multivariate time series (MTS) forecasting problem. While canonical deep learning sequence models like LSTM networks [7] and modern Transformer variants [15] have demonstrated efficacy, they encounter limitations in this specific domain. Specifically, predicting long-term GSTA requires preserving geophysical periodic priors (e.g., precise daily and annual cycles dictated by Earth's orbit) and capturing the dynamic, asymmetric inter-dependencies among diverse globally-averaged atmospheric variables (e.g., surface solar radiation, specific humidity, and varying anthropogenic $\text{CO}_2$ concentrations). Purely self-attention-based or recurrent mechanisms often struggle to explicitly encode these rigid periodicities and structural variable dependencies without incurring high computational costs.

To explicitly address these complex temporal variations and variable interactions, this study reconceptualizes GSTA forecasting through a dual-graph perspective. Earth’s rotation and orbit dictate observable intra-period (daily) and inter-period (annual) temperature fluctuations. Simultaneously, atmospheric variables are physically coupled, forming an implicit interaction network.

To integrate these geophysical priors with data-driven modeling, we propose the Multivariate Multi-period Temperature Time Series Graph Neural Network (MTTGNet). The main contributions of this study are summarized as follows:

* We formulate GSTA forecasting as a multi-scale climate dynamics modeling problem, where both short-term climate variability and long-term periodic evolution are explicitly represented through temporal graph structures.
* We develop a multi-period climate representation framework that separately models daily and yearly climate recurrences, enabling the preservation of geophysical periodicity across different temporal scales.
* We introduce an atmospheric interaction learning mechanism to dynamically capture nonlinear dependencies among multiple meteorological variables, including radiation, pressure, cloud cover, precipitation, wind components, and atmospheric $\text{CO}_2$ concentration.
* We demonstrate through experiments on ERA5 and NOAA datasets that explicitly modeling climate periodicity and atmospheric interactions improves forecasting performance and enables analysis of different $\text{CO}_2$ emission scenarios.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 MTS Forecasting
The field of MTS forecasting has undergone structural evolution. Initially, canonical recurrent networks such as LSTM [7] and GRU [8] provided the foundational paradigm for capturing temporal continuity. Subsequently, the success of attention mechanisms ushered in the Transformer era for time series [16]. Models like Informer [17] and Autoformer [18] addressed long-term dependencies and basic seasonal decompositions, setting new forecasting benchmarks.

As the demand for processing longer sequences grew, recent literature witnessed a structural paradigm shift. PatchTST [19] segmented time series into subseries patches and utilized channel-independence. Concurrently, TimesNet [15] transformed 1D sequences into 2D tensors based on multi-periodicities, while iTransformer [20] inverted the conventional Transformer dimensions to model multivariate correlations. More recently, the community has explored efficient non-Transformer architectures. For instance, ModernTCN [21] revitalized pure convolutional structures, TimeMixer [22] proposed multi-scale MLP architectures, and models like TimeMachine [23] and PGN [24] leveraged state-space models and modern recurrent designs. Despite this architectural evolution, purely data-driven sequence models often struggle to intrinsically enforce rigid geophysical cycles (e.g., exact daily and annual periodicities) or capture dynamic inter-variable correlations specific to meteorological environments [25]. This limitation highlights the necessity of integrating physical graph priors into the forecasting architecture.

### 2.2 GNNs for Time Series and Climate Modeling
GNNs have extended to time series by conceptualizing sequence data as dynamic graphs. Early spatiotemporal models, such as DCRNN [26] and Graph WaveNet [27], demonstrated potential but primarily relied on predefined, fixed sensor topologies. For general MTS forecasting where explicit spatial topology is absent (such as globally-averaged climate indicators), adaptive graph learning became essential. Models like MTGNN [28] and AGCRN [29] learned implicit adjacency matrices to capture cross-variable dependencies.

More recently, contemporary GNNs emphasize the explicit decoupling of temporal ordering from variable interactions, as seen in frameworks like HSDGNN [30] and $S^2$-GNN [31]. In the specialized domain of climate and weather forecasting, GNNs have proven capable of extracting nonlinear physical relationships. For instance, recent studies have introduced graph node training approaches designed for predicting global average temperature series [32], as well as dynamic multi-scale networks with multivariate interaction for probabilistic sea surface temperature forecasting [33].

Inspired by this evolutionary trajectory and structural decoupling principles, our MTTGNet separates the forecasting problem into an attention-driven Variable Graph (extracting physical driver interactions) and Multi-period Temporal Graphs (enforcing daily and yearly sequential evolution).
